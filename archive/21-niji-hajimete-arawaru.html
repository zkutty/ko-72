Spring  ·  Micro-season 21 of 72  ·  Apr 15

First rainbows appear

Niji hajimete arawaru  ·  虹始見

After a brief spring shower, the air holds a crystalline stillness, and there — arching over the cedar-dark mountains — the first rainbow of the year materializes like a half-remembered dream. The light has shifted into something new: no longer the pale wash of early spring but a luminous clarity that makes every wet leaf gleam. One can smell the earth breathing, petrichor mingling with the green scent of bamboo shoots pushing through damp soil.

Nature Notes

The swallows have fully claimed the eaves now, their chittering a constant underscore to the lengthening days. Cherry blossoms have mostly fallen, but in their wake the yamabuki (kerria) blazes golden along stream banks and temple walls. Frogs have begun their evening chorus in the rice paddies, and the afternoon skies build tall cumulus clouds that release brief, warm rains — the very rains that conjure these first rainbows.

At the Table

Takenoko gohan

Rice cooked with fresh bamboo shoots, their sweetness most fleeting and prized in these very days before the shoots toughen.

Sawara no saikyo-yaki

Spanish mackerel marinated in sweet white miso and grilled until caramelized — sawara meaning 'spring-fish' in its characters, the kanji itself an instruction to eat it now.

Cultural Note

This period falls within the Seimei festival season, when families visit ancestral graves to clean the stones and offer fresh flowers — a practice called haka-mairi. The name Seimei itself means 'pure brightness,' describing both the clarified atmosphere and the spiritual clarity sought through honoring one's lineage. In some regions, people believe rainbows during this time are bridges the spirits use to return briefly to the living world.

虹立ちて村の時計の鳴り渡る

niji tachite mura no tokei no nari wataru

A rainbow rises — across the village, the clock tower chimes through the clearing air

The rainbow fades as quietly as it came, leaving only the dripping eaves and a silence that feels, somehow, more spacious than before.
