Crow-dipper sprouts.

Hange shōzu

The humid air thickens as crow-dipper emerges from wet soil, marking the deepest pause of early summer before the rains withdraw.

The earth exhales moisture. In shaded corners of old gardens, the pale spike of crow-dipper — half-summer herb — pushes through black soil, its hooded flower strange and otherworldly. The air is heavy with the scent of wet stone and rotting leaves, and even at midday the light seems to carry a greenish weight.

Nature notes

Frogs call continuously from flooded paddies, their chorus rising and falling through the night. Hydrangeas have deepened to their final blues and purples, petals beginning to brown at the edges. The rice seedlings, now firmly rooted, stand in perfect rows beneath a sky that rarely shows its full face — clouds drift and reform, threatening rain that may or may not fall.

Cultural note

Hange is considered an inauspicious day — one of the sanrinbō, when field and garden work is forbidden lest the crops fail. Farmers rest, mend tools, and prepare offerings. In Kansai, eating octopus on this day remains a living custom, the creature's tenacious grip invoked to help rice roots take firm hold in the paddies.
