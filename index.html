THE YEAR · 七十二候

Spring Summer Autumn Winter
T

he air hangs thick with moisture, and along the edges of ponds and slow canals, irises unfurl their purple flags. Their petals, delicate as silk crepe, hold droplets from the morning mist. The scent of wet earth and green stems rises where one walks among them, and the light filtering through clouds gives every color a softened, almost underwater quality.

Nature

Fireflies begin their brief, flickering courtship over rice paddies and stream banks in the evenings. Hydrangeas deepen from pale lavender to indigo as the soil's acidity shifts with the rains. Tree frogs call persistently from dusk onward, their chorus a pulse beneath the humid nights.

Table

. Clear broth with pike conger and junsai water shield, its elegant simplicity cooling the palate as summer heat builds.

. Sweetfish simmered until the bones soften in sweet soy, a preservation method that honors the river's brief abundance.

. Fragrant green shiso leaves wrapped around seasoned rice or pickles, their bright herbaceous bite cutting through humid-weather fatigue.

. Young soybeans boiled in salted water, their grassy sweetness at its peak before the pods toughen.

Rite

Iris viewing, or hanashōbu, draws visitors to famous gardens such as Meiji Shrine's inner garden and Horikiri in Tokyo. Unlike cherry blossoms' fleeting days, irises bloom in succession for weeks, inviting contemplative return visits. The flower's association with purification and warding off illness dates to the ancient Tango no Sekku customs, when iris leaves were floated in baths.
