Lotus blossoms open

蓮始開

Hasu hajimete hiraku

THE YEAR · 七十二候

Spring Summer Autumn Winter
I

n the heavy stillness before sunrise, lotus buds that have waited all night begin to open with an almost audible sigh. The air above temple ponds grows thick with their honeyed scent, mingling with the green smell of paddy water. By midmorning, the blossoms will close again — this brief flowering belongs only to those who rise early.

Nature

Dragonflies patrol the lotus ponds in territorial circuits, their wings catching the morning light like flecks of mica. Tree frogs call from the shade of broad lotus leaves, their voices carrying far across the water. The summer heat has settled in fully now, and cicadas begin their chorus earlier each day, the sound building in waves through the humid afternoons.

Table

. Lotus root julienned and sautéed with sesame — its crisp, lace-like slices echo the season's flower.

. Okinawan stir-fry of bitter melon with tofu and pork, a cooling dish that fights summer fatigue.

. Winter melon simmered with minced chicken in dashi, the pale flesh turning translucent and tender.

. Lacy tempura fritters of whitebait, capturing the delicate sweetness of summer's small fish.

Rite

At Shinobazu Pond in Ueno and lotus-viewing sites across Japan, early risers gather for kanren — lotus appreciation. The Japanese have long associated the lotus with spiritual purity, its blossoms rising immaculate from murky water. Buddhist temples hold special dawn viewings, and some estates serve lotus tea, brewed by pouring water through the dew-filled leaves.
